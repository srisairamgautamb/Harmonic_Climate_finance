# Phase 2 – Data Architecture for the Spectral Climate–Financial Transmission Framework

## 1. Objectives and Scope

Phase 2 defines how real-world climate, socio-economic, and financial datasets are mapped into the abstract processes \(D_t, C_t, R_t^{\mathrm{cl}}, R_t^{\mathrm{fin}}\) introduced in Phase 1, with a focus on temporal alignment, transformation to approximate covariance stationarity, and construction of clean matrices suitable for spectral analysis. The goal is a mathematically precise description of operators that take raw, heterogeneous time series and output aligned, transformed series indexed on a common discrete time grid (e.g. monthly) over a chosen horizon (e.g. 1990–2023).[1][2]

## 2. Time Grid and Indexing

### 2.1 Common time grid

Let the analysis be conducted on a discrete time grid \(t = 1,\dots,T\) representing equally spaced calendar periods (e.g. months) over a chosen horizon (e.g. January 1990–December 2023). Monthly resolution is a natural compromise between the low frequency of many climate indicators and the higher frequency of financial data, and is widely used in climate–macro and climate–energy applications.[3][1]

Define a bijection between calendar dates and integer indices via a mapping 
\[ \tau: \{1,\dots,T\} \to \{\text{calendar months in horizon}\}. \]
All processes \(D_t, C_t, R_t^{\mathrm{cl}}, R_t^{\mathrm{fin}}\) will be defined on this grid after resampling and aggregation.

### 2.2 Raw observation times and alignment operator

Each raw series is observed at times \(s \in \mathcal{S}_j\) that may be irregular or at different frequencies (daily, monthly, quarterly, annual). Let \(X^{\mathrm{raw}}_j(s)\) denote the raw value of variable \(j\) at raw time \(s\). Define an **alignment operator** \(\mathcal{A}_j\) that maps raw observations to the common grid:
\[
X_{j,t} := (\mathcal{A}_j X^{\mathrm{raw}}_j)_t = g_j\bigl(\{X^{\mathrm{raw}}_j(s): s \in W_j(t)\}\bigr),
\]
where \(W_j(t)\) is a window of raw times associated with calendar period \(\tau(t)\) (e.g. all daily observations within that month) and \(g_j\) is an aggregation or interpolation functional (e.g. average, end-of-period, sum depending on the economic meaning).[3]

The operator \(\mathcal{A}_j\) is chosen such that the resulting \(X_{j,t}\) respects the natural temporal aggregation of the underlying process (e.g. average temperature anomaly over the month, last trading-day VIX for the month).

## 3. Variable Blocks and Data Matrices

### 3.1 Socio-economic drivers \(D_t\)

Suppose there are \(p\) driver series indexed by \(j = 1,\dots,p\), drawn from demographic, energy, land-use, geopolitical, and emissions data. After alignment, define[2][4]
\[
D_t := (D_{1,t},\dots,D_{p,t})^{\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{p}, \quad t = 1,\dots,T.
\]
Stacking across time gives the driver data matrix
\[
\mathbf{D} :=
\begin{pmatrix}
D_1^{\top} \\
\vdots \\
D_T^{\top}
\end{pmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^{T \times p}.
\]

### 3.2 Climate state variables \(C_t\)

Let \(m\) climate state series be selected (e.g. global temperature anomaly, ENSO indices, ocean heat content, drought indices, sea-level rise), primarily from authoritative climate archives such as NOAA, NASA, and Copernicus. After alignment to the monthly grid, define[5][1]
\[
C_t := (C_{1,t},\dots,C_{m,t})^{\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}, \quad
\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times m}
\]
as above.

### 3.3 Climate risk index \(R_t^{\mathrm{cl}}\)

Climate risk variables may be constructed indices (e.g. national or global climate risk indices, disaster-loss metrics, policy-uncertainty measures, carbon price volatility). Sources such as the Germanwatch Climate Risk Index, EM-DAT loss data, and NGFS scenario outputs provide candidate inputs. For generality, allow a vector of climate risk indices of dimension \(q_{\mathrm{cl}}\):[6][7][8]
\[
R_t^{\mathrm{cl}} := (R^{\mathrm{cl}}_{1,t},\dots,R^{\mathrm{cl}}_{q_{\mathrm{cl}},t})^{\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{q_{\mathrm{cl}}},
\quad \mathbf{R}^{\mathrm{cl}} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times q_{\mathrm{cl}}}.
\]

### 3.4 Financial risk indicators \(R_t^{\mathrm{fin}}\)

Financial risk variables (e.g. VIX, credit spreads, CDS spreads, bank equity volatility, energy-sector returns) are sourced from financial databases (FRED, Bloomberg, WRDS, or equivalents). These are typically available at daily or higher frequencies and thus require temporal aggregation to the monthly grid via operators \(\mathcal{A}_j\) as in Section 2.2. Let the dimension of the financial risk vector be \(q_{\mathrm{fin}}\):[9][7]
\[
R_t^{\mathrm{fin}} := (R^{\mathrm{fin}}_{1,t},\dots,R^{\mathrm{fin}}_{q_{\mathrm{fin}},t})^{\top} \in \mathbb{R}^{q_{\mathrm{fin}}},
\quad \mathbf{R}^{\mathrm{fin}} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times q_{\mathrm{fin}}}.
\]

### 3.5 Full data matrix

Define the full stacked matrix
\[
\mathbf{Z} := \bigl[\,\mathbf{D} \mid \mathbf{C} \mid \mathbf{R}^{\mathrm{cl}} \mid \mathbf{R}^{\mathrm{fin}}\,\bigr] \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d},
\]
where \(d = p + m + q_{\mathrm{cl}} + q_{\mathrm{fin}}\). Row \(t\) of \(\mathbf{Z}\) corresponds to the vector \(Z_t\) defined in Phase 1.

## 4. Scaling, Transformation, and Approximate Stationarity

### 4.1 Logarithmic and power transformations

Variables strictly positive in level (e.g. GDP, emissions, energy use, price indices) are often transformed using logs or other power transforms to stabilize variance and render growth rates additive:
\[
X^{(\log)}_{j,t} := \log X_{j,t}, \quad \text{or} \quad X^{(\lambda)}_{j,t} := \frac{X_{j,t}^{\lambda} - 1}{\lambda}.
\]
This is standard practice in time-series econometrics to improve stationarity properties and interpretability of differences as growth rates.[10]

### 4.2 Seasonal adjustment and differencing operators

Many macro, climate, and financial series exhibit strong seasonality (e.g. energy demand, precipitation, agricultural production). Seasonal adjustment and differencing operators are applied to remove deterministic seasonal patterns and low-frequency trends:[11][12][10]

- Seasonal differencing with period \(s\) (e.g. \(s=12\) for monthly data):
  \[ \Delta_s X_{t} := X_t - X_{t-s}. \]
- First differencing:
  \[ \Delta X_t := X_t - X_{t-1}. \]
- Combined transformation for a given series:
  \[ \tilde X_t := \Delta^{k_1} \Delta_s^{k_2} T(X_t), \]
  where \(T\) is a variance-stabilizing transform (log or power), and \(k_1,k_2 \in \{0,1\}\) are chosen based on stationarity diagnostics.

Let \(\mathcal{S}_X\) denote the composite operator that maps the aligned raw series to the transformed stationary proxy:
\[
\tilde X_t := (\mathcal{S}_X X)_t.
\]

### 4.3 Standardization

For each transformed variable \(\tilde X_j\), compute the sample mean and standard deviation over the training window:
\[ \hat \mu_j = \frac{1}{T_0} \sum_{t=1}^{T_0} \tilde X_{j,t}, \quad
   \hat \sigma_j^2 = \frac{1}{T_0-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T_0} (\tilde X_{j,t} - \hat \mu_j)^2. \]
Then define standardized variables
\[ \hat X_{j,t} := \frac{\tilde X_{j,t} - \hat \mu_j}{\hat \sigma_j}. \]
This yields approximately unit-variance, zero-mean series, which is beneficial both for spectral density estimation and for the conditioning of subsequent machine-learning models (classical and quantum).[11][10]

Define a diagonal scaling operator \(\mathcal{N}: \mathbb{R}^{T \times d} \to \mathbb{R}^{T \times d}\) that applies this standardization column-wise.

## 5. Missing-Data Handling

### 5.1 Missingness patterns

Climate and socio-economic datasets often feature irregular missingness due to measurement gaps, country coverage variation, or sensor outages, while financial series may have holidays or market closures. Missingness patterns can be structured (e.g. no data before a launch date) or sporadic (occasional gaps).[8][2]

### 5.2 Imputation operators

Let \(M_{j,t} \in \{0,1\}\) indicate whether the aligned observation \(X_{j,t}\) is observed (1) or missing (0). A generic **imputation operator** \(\mathcal{I}_j\) acts on a partially observed series to produce a complete one:
\[
X^{\mathrm{imp}}_{j,t} := (\mathcal{I}_j X)_t.
\]

Choices for \(\mathcal{I}_j\) include:

- Deterministic interpolation (e.g. linear, spline) within short gaps.
- Carry-forward or carry-backward for isolated missing values in financial series when the economic meaning supports it (e.g. last observation carried forward over holidays).
- Model-based imputation using cross-sectional information (e.g. regression on related variables or EM-type algorithms) for larger gaps.[2][8]

The operator \(\mathcal{I}_j\) is chosen to minimize distortion of the spectral structure, favoring methods that avoid introducing spurious high-frequency components.

### 5.3 Masking for learning

In addition to imputation, a binary mask matrix \(\mathbf{M} \in \{0,1\}^{T \times d}\) is retained to record original missingness, which can later be used in model estimation (e.g. as weights or inputs) to avoid overconfidence in heavily imputed regions.

## 6. Composite Data-Preparation Operator

For each variable \(j\), define the composite operator
\[
\mathcal{P}_j := \mathcal{N}_j \circ \mathcal{S}_j \circ \mathcal{I}_j \circ \mathcal{A}_j,
\]
where:

- \(\mathcal{A}_j\) aligns raw data to the common time grid.
- \(\mathcal{I}_j\) imputes missing values.
- \(\mathcal{S}_j\) applies transformations to approximate stationarity.
- \(\mathcal{N}_j\) standardizes.

Applying this pipeline column-wise to the full raw data matrix \(\mathbf{Z}^{\mathrm{raw}}\) yields the final analysis-ready matrix
\[
\hat{\mathbf{Z}} := \mathcal{P}(\mathbf{Z}^{\mathrm{raw}}) \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d},
\]
whose rows correspond to the transformed, approximately stationary vectors \(\hat Z_t\) used in Phase 3 for spectral analysis.

## 7. Stationarity Diagnostics and Sub-Sampling

### 7.1 Diagnostic tests

While \(\mathcal{S}_j\) is designed to induce approximate covariance stationarity, formal tests (e.g. ADF, KPSS) and visual diagnostics (ACF, periodograms) should be applied to each column of \(\hat{\mathbf{Z}}\) to verify stationarity over the chosen horizon. Nonstationary residual structure (e.g. structural breaks, changing variance) may motivate additional transformations or restriction to subperiods.[10][11]

### 7.2 Local stationarity and regime splits

If there is compelling evidence of regime shifts (e.g. pre- and post-major climate policy changes or financial crises), the time axis may be partitioned into regimes \(\mathcal{T}_1,\dots,\mathcal{T}_R\) and separate data matrices \(\hat{\mathbf{Z}}^{(r)}\) constructed for each regime. This supports both global spectral analysis over the full sample and local spectral analysis within regimes, as well as potential comparisons across regimes.[7][5]

## 8. Mapping Back to Phase 1 Objects

Given the transformed, aligned, and standardized matrix \(\hat{\mathbf{Z}}\), define the abstract processes from Phase 1 via
\[
\hat Z_t := \hat{\mathbf{Z}}_{t,\cdot}^{\top} =
\begin{pmatrix}
\hat D_t \\
\hat C_t \\
\hat R_t^{\mathrm{cl}} \\
\hat R_t^{\mathrm{fin}}
\end{pmatrix}, \quad t = 1,\dots,T.
\]
Here \(\hat D_t, \hat C_t, \hat R_t^{\mathrm{cl}}, \hat R_t^{\mathrm{fin}}\) inherit their dimensionality and ordering from the columns of \(\hat{\mathbf{Z}}\) corresponding to the respective blocks. For notational simplicity, subsequent phases may drop hats and assume all time-domain processes refer to the transformed versions, with stationarity and scaling implicitly understood.

This completes the mathematical data architecture for Phase 2: a set of operators and matrix constructions that translate heterogeneous raw climate, socio-economic, and financial data into a unified, analysis-ready representation suitable for the spectral transformations and quantum harmonic learning developed in later phases.[7][8][2]

## 2. Phase 3 – Spectral and Harmonic Transformation

### 2.1 Windowed discrete Fourier transform

Given the transformed, approximately stationary multivariate process \(\hat Z_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\) for \(t = 1,\dots,T\), define a sliding-window discrete Fourier transform (DFT) to capture local spectral structure. For a window length \(L\) (e.g. 10–20 years of monthly data, \(L \approx 120\text{–}240\)), and step size \(\Delta\) (e.g. 1–3 months), define window centers \(t_c\) such that the window \(W(t_c) = \{t_c - L/2 + 1,\dots,t_c + L/2\}\) lies within \(1,\dots,T\).

For each variable \(j \in \{1,\dots,d\}\) and window center \(t_c\), define the tapered DFT:
\[
\hat Z_j(\omega_k; t_c) = \sum_{\ell=0}^{L-1} w_\ell\, \hat Z_{j, t_c - L/2 + 1 + \ell}\, e^{-i \omega_k \ell},
\]
where \(w_\ell\) is a taper (e.g. Hanning) and \(\omega_k = 2\pi k / L\), \(k = 0,\dots, L-1\).[1]

Collect these into a complex-valued vector for each \(t_c\):
\[
\hat Z(\omega_k; t_c) := (\hat Z_1(\omega_k; t_c),\dots,\hat Z_d(\omega_k; t_c))^{\top} \in \mathbb{C}^{d}.
\]

### 2.2 Spectral density and cross-spectral matrices

For global (full-sample) spectral quantities, estimate the spectral density matrix using a standard multivariate periodogram smoothed over frequencies:
\[
I_Z(\omega_k) = \frac{1}{2\pi T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \hat Z_t e^{-i \omega_k t} \sum_{s=1}^{T} \hat Z_s^{\top} e^{i \omega_k s},
\]
\[
\hat S_Z(\omega_k) = \sum_{\ell=-L_S}^{L_S} K\bigl(\ell / L_S\bigr) I_Z(\omega_{k+\ell}),
\]
where \(K\) is a smoothing kernel and \(L_S\) a bandwidth parameter.[4]

The cross-spectral density between any two subvectors (e.g. climate risk and financial risk) is the corresponding block of \(\hat S_Z(\omega_k)\):
\[
\hat S_{R^{\mathrm{cl}} R^{\mathrm{fin}}}(\omega_k) \in \mathbb{C}^{q_{\mathrm{cl}} \times q_{\mathrm{fin}}}.
\]

### 2.3 Coherence and phase spectra

For scalar components \(X_t, Y_t\) extracted from \(\hat Z_t\), the squared coherence at frequency \(\omega_k\) is
\[
\gamma_{XY}^2(\omega_k) = \frac{|\hat S_{XY}(\omega_k)|^2}{\hat S_{XX}(\omega_k)\,\hat S_{YY}(\omega_k)},
\]
with phase spectrum 
\[
\phi_{XY}(\omega_k) = \arg\bigl(\hat S_{XY}(\omega_k)\bigr).
\]
These functions capture, respectively, the normalized coupling strength and lead–lag relationships at different frequencies.[2][4]

### 2.4 Harmonic structure and combination frequencies

Climate dynamics often exhibit harmonics (e.g. annual and semi-annual cycles) and combination frequencies (e.g. ENSO plus annual cycle) that appear as branches in the spectral decomposition of dynamical operators. To explicitly track these, define a set of **fundamental frequencies** \(\{\nu_r\}\) (e.g. annual, ENSO, quasi-decadal), and consider their harmonics and combinations:[3][1]
\[
\Omega_{\mathrm{harm}} = \{ m \nu_r : m = 1,2,\dots, M_r,\, r = 1,\dots,R\},
\]
\[
\Omega_{\mathrm{comb}} = \{ \nu_r \pm \nu_{r'} : r \neq r' \},
\]
then map these to discrete frequency indices \(k\) via \(\omega_k \approx 2\pi \nu\). Spectral features will be constructed to emphasize these harmonic sets.

### 2.5 Spectral feature matrices for learning

Define a selected set of \(K\) frequencies (including harmonics/combination modes): \(\Omega_{\mathrm{sel}} = \{\omega_{k_1},\dots,\omega_{k_K}\}\). For each time index or window center \(t_c\), build a **spectral feature vector** by concatenating magnitudes and (optionally) phases:
\[
\phi(t_c) = \Bigl( 
\bigl|\hat U(\omega_{k_1}; t_c)\bigr|,\dots,\bigl|\hat U(\omega_{k_K}; t_c)\bigr|,\,
\operatorname{Re}\hat U(\omega_{k_1}; t_c),\dots,\operatorname{Im}\hat U(\omega_{k_K}; t_c)
\Bigr) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\phi},
\]
where \(\hat U(\omega; t_c)\) stacks the upstream blocks (\(D,C,R^{\mathrm{cl}}\)) and \(|\cdot|\) is elementwise magnitude. These \(\phi(t_c)\) are the inputs for both classical and quantum models in later phases.

## 3. Phase 4 – Spectral and Harmonic Causality Framework

### 3.1 Multivariate spectral Granger causality

For a VAR representation of \(\hat Z_t\) or an estimated spectral density \(\hat S_Z(\omega)\), frequency-domain Granger causality from block \(X\) to block \(Y\) (conditional on other variables) can be defined via a spectral factorization of \(S_Z(\omega)\) into transfer and innovation covariance matrices.[5][4]

Assume for exposition that \(\hat Z_t\) follows a stable VAR(p):
\[
\hat Z_t = \sum_{\ell=1}^{p} A_\ell \hat Z_{t-\ell} + \eta_t, \quad \mathbb{E}[\eta_t \eta_t^{\top}] = \Sigma_\eta.
\]
The corresponding **harmonic transfer matrix** is
\[
H(\omega) = \Bigl(I_d - \sum_{\ell=1}^{p} A_\ell e^{-i \omega \ell}\Bigr)^{-1},
\]
with spectral density
\[
S_Z(\omega) = H(\omega) \, \Sigma_\eta \, H(\omega)^{*}.
\]

Partition \(Z_t\) into \(X_t\) and \(Y_t\) plus conditioning variables, and denote corresponding blocks of \(H(\omega)\) and \(\Sigma_\eta\). The **spectral Granger causality** from \(X\) to \(Y\) at frequency \(\omega\) can be expressed as a log-ratio of conditional spectra (Geweke-style measures):
\[
F_{X \to Y}(\omega) = \log \frac{|S_{Y|\text{others}}(\omega)|}{|S_{Y|X,\text{others}}(\omega)|},
\]
where the denominators and numerators correspond to spectral densities under restricted and unrestricted models.[4][5]

### 3.2 Harmonic Granger causality with external driving

Classical spectral Granger causality may fail to detect causal influence at externally driven harmonic frequencies when the driving is exogenous to the AR structure; harmonic-augmented causality methods augment the model with explicit oscillatory terms to capture such effects.[2]

Introduce an **environmental harmonic driver** \(E_t\) (e.g. an exogenous oscillatory mode or policy cycle), possibly represented in spectral form via known or estimated frequencies \(\omega^{(E)}_h\). Extend the VAR to include harmonic regressors for \(E_t\):
\[
\hat Z_t = \sum_{\ell=1}^{p} A_\ell \hat Z_{t-\ell} + \sum_{h=1}^{H} B_h 
\begin{pmatrix}
\cos(\omega^{(E)}_h t) \\
\sin(\omega^{(E)}_h t)
\end{pmatrix}
+ \eta_t.
\]

In frequency domain, this induces additional peaks at the harmonic driving frequencies. Define a **harmonic Granger causality** from \(X\) to \(Y\) at harmonic frequency \(\omega^{(E)}_h\) by comparing models with and without the interaction between \(X\) and the harmonic driver, ensuring that externally imposed oscillations are properly accounted for.[2]

### 3.3 Long-term and low-frequency causality

Low-frequency or “long-term” causality, relevant for climate change and slow macro–financial adjustment, can be quantified by integrating spectral causality measures over low-frequency bands near zero. Define a low-frequency band \(B_{\mathrm{LF}} = [0, \omega_{\mathrm{LF}}]\). Then[6]
\[
F^{\mathrm{LF}}_{X \to Y} = \int_{0}^{\omega_{\mathrm{LF}}} F_{X \to Y}(\omega) \, d\omega.
\]
Similarly, high-frequency bands can capture fast market responses to short-term climate shocks.

### 3.4 Climate–finance spectral transmission network

For each selected frequency \(\omega_k\), define a directed weighted network with nodes corresponding to variables in \(D_t, C_t, R_t^{\mathrm{cl}}, R_t^{\mathrm{fin}}\). Define edge weights using directional spectral measures:

- From variable \(i\) to \(j\):
  \[ w_{i \to j}(\omega_k) := \max\bigl\{F_{i \to j}(\omega_k), 0\bigr\}. \]

Organize these into frequency-specific adjacency matrices \(W(\omega_k)\). Layered structure (drivers, climate state, climate risk, financial risk) is encoded by grouping nodes and constraining or interpreting edges accordingly. The collection \(\{W(\omega_k)\}_{k=1}^{K}\) defines a **multi-layer harmonic spectral transmission network** characterizing how information flows across the climate–finance system at different frequencies and their harmonics.[7][1]

## 4. Phase 5 – Novel Harmonic-Spectral Transmission Model

Phase 5 moves beyond standard spectral VAR/Granger methods by introducing new mathematical constructs: a **Harmonic Spectral Transmission Operator**, a **Spectral–Harmonic Propagation Kernel**, and a **Spectral–Koopman-inspired climate–finance operator**.

### 4.1 Harmonic Spectral Transmission Operator

Recall the upstream spectral vector \(\hat U(\omega) = (\hat D(\omega), \hat C(\omega), \hat R^{\mathrm{cl}}(\omega))\) and downstream financial vector \(\hat R^{\mathrm{fin}}(\omega)\). Define the **Harmonic Spectral Transmission Operator** (HSTO)
\[
\mathcal{T}_H(\omega) : \mathbb{C}^{d_U} \to \mathbb{C}^{q_{\mathrm{fin}}}
\]
such that
\[
\hat R^{\mathrm{fin}}(\omega) = \mathcal{T}_H(\omega)\,\hat U(\omega) + \hat \xi(\omega),
\]
with \(\hat \xi(\omega)\) orthogonal (in expectation) to \(\hat U(\omega)\) at each \(\omega\). Unlike a standard transfer function, \(\mathcal{T}_H(\omega)\) is explicitly **harmonic-structured**:
\[
\mathcal{T}_H(\omega) = \sum_{h \in \mathcal{H}} \Phi_h(\omega) \, K_h,
\]
where

- \(\mathcal{H}\) indexes harmonic families (fundamental, harmonics, combination bands).
- \(\Phi_h(\omega)\) are scalar or diagonal weighting functions sharply localized around harmonic branches (e.g. Gaussian windows around \(\Omega_{\mathrm{harm}}\) and \(\Omega_{\mathrm{comb}}\)).
- \(K_h \in \mathbb{C}^{q_{\mathrm{fin}} \times d_U}\) are complex matrices encoding cross-layer coupling patterns specific to each harmonic family.

This decomposition separates climate–finance transmission into interpretable harmonic channels (e.g. annual cycle effects, ENSO–annual combination modes) and allows one to study robustness or regime shifts in each channel separately.[3][1]

### 4.2 Spectral–Harmonic Propagation Kernel

Define a **Spectral–Harmonic Propagation Kernel** (SHPK) that maps spectral distributions of upstream variables to conditional distributions of financial risk across frequencies. For each frequency pair \((\omega, \omega')\), introduce
\[
K_{\mathrm{SH}}(\omega, \omega') : \mathbb{C}^{d_U} \times \mathbb{C}^{d_U} \to \mathbb{R},
\]
with a proposed structure
\[
K_{\mathrm{SH}}(\omega, \omega') = 
\kappa\bigl(\hat U(\omega), \hat U(\omega')\bigr) \, \rho(\omega, \omega'),
\]
where:

- \(\kappa\) is a positive-definite kernel on spectral vectors (e.g. complex Gaussian, phase-only kernel).
- \(\rho(\omega, \omega')\) is a harmonic correlation measure that is large when \(\omega\) and \(\omega'\) lie on the same harmonic branch or combination path.

The SHPK thus encodes both similarity of spectral content and harmonic proximity, enabling learning algorithms to generalize across related frequencies (e.g. between annual and semiannual cycles) and to capture cross-frequency couplings.

### 4.3 Spectral–Koopman climate–finance operator (conceptual)

Building on operator-theoretic methods for climate dynamics (Koopman/transfer operators), one can interpret \(H(\omega)\) and \(\mathcal{T}_H(\omega)\) as finite-dimensional approximations of a **climate–finance Koopman operator** acting on observables that mix climate and financial variables. Let \(\Phi\) be a space of observables on the joint state space of climate and financial variables, and define a (discrete-time) Koopman operator \(\mathcal{U}\) by[1]
\[ (\mathcal{U} f)(z) = f(F(z)), \]
where \(F\) is the underlying (unknown) dynamics. In spectral coordinates, eigenfunctions of \(\mathcal{U}\) at eigenvalues \(e^{i\omega}\) capture coherent oscillatory modes such as ENSO and their financial manifestations.

In this project, \(\mathcal{T}_H(\omega)\) can be seen as a **projection of the Koopman spectrum** onto financial observables, restricted to harmonically significant branches. This provides a principled justification for focusing on harmonic decomposition and for using spectral eigendata as inputs to machine-learning models.

### 4.4 Identification and estimation strategy (high level)

- Estimate \(\hat S_Z(\omega)\) and fit a VAR or directly factorize \(\hat S_Z(\omega)\) using algorithms such as Wilson’s method to obtain a transfer matrix \(\hat H(\omega)\).[4]
- Identify harmonic branches (annual, ENSO, low-frequency) using peaks in \(\hat S_Z(\omega)\) and/or data-driven operator-theoretic modes.[3][1]
- For each harmonic family, estimate matrices \(K_h\) in \(\mathcal{T}_H(\omega)\) by regressing \(\hat R^{\mathrm{fin}}(\omega)\) on \(\hat U(\omega)\) using regularized complex-valued regression, with \(\Phi_h(\omega)\) as known windows.
- Combine harmonic contributions to recover a full transmission operator \(\mathcal{T}_H(\omega)\) over \(\Omega_{\mathrm{sel}}\).

This design yields a new, harmonically resolved structural object that subsumes classical transfer functions and provides richer interpretability for climate–finance channels.

## 5. Phase 6 – Quantum Harmonic Embedding

Phase 6 defines a quantum feature map tailored to harmonic spectral features, inspired by Spectral Phase Encoding (SPE) and quantum time-series encodings.[8][9][10]

### 5.1 Classical spectral front-end

Start from the spectral feature vectors \(\phi(t_c) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\phi}\) constructed in Phase 3, focusing on a subset of harmonically relevant frequencies and variables (primarily upstream \(D, C, R^{\mathrm{cl}}\)). Optionally, apply a linear dimensionality reduction (e.g. PCA in spectral space) to obtain a reduced vector \(x(t_c) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_x}\) with \(d_x \le d_\phi\), preserving harmonically important components.

### 5.2 Complex-valued spectral representation

Let \(F(x(t_c)) \in \mathbb{C}^{m}\) be a truncated complex DFT (or another suitable spectral transform) of \(x(t_c)\):
\[
F(x)_k = \sum_{j=1}^{d_x} x_j e^{-i 2\pi jk / d_x}, \quad k = 0,\dots,m-1.
\]
Extract phases and normalized magnitudes:
\[
\theta_k = \arg F(x)_k, \quad r_k = \frac{|F(x)_k|}{\max_{\ell} |F(x)_\ell| + \epsilon}.
\]

### 5.3 Diagonal phase-only quantum embedding (harmonic SPE)

Consider an \(n\)-qubit Hilbert space with dimension \(2^n = m\). Initialize a uniform superposition state
\[
|\psi_0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{m}} \sum_{k=0}^{m-1} |k\rangle.
\]
Define a diagonal unitary depending on \(x(t_c)\):
\[
U_{\mathrm{HPE}}(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{m-1} e^{i f_k(\theta_k, r_k)} |k\rangle\langle k|,
\]
where \(f_k\) are encoding functions that incorporate both phase and magnitude, for example
\[
f_k(\theta_k, r_k) = \alpha \, \theta_k + \beta \, r_k,
\]
with tunable hyperparameters \(\alpha, \beta\). The resulting **quantum harmonic embedding state** is
\[
|\psi(x)\rangle = U_{\mathrm{HPE}}(x) |\psi_0\rangle.
\]
This construction directly parallels Spectral Phase Encoding but specialized to harmonically curated climate–finance spectral features.[8]

### 5.4 Quantum kernel and QSVM/QKR

The quantum kernel between two inputs \(x, x'\) is
\[
K_Q(x, x') = |\langle \psi(x) | \psi(x') \rangle|^2.
\]
Given the diagonal structure of \(U_{\mathrm{HPE}}\), this overlaps simplifies to
\[
\langle \psi(x) | \psi(x') \rangle = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{k=0}^{m-1} e^{i (f_k(\theta_k, r_k) - f_k(\theta'_k, r'_k))}.
\]
This is a **harmonic kernel** on spectral features, sensitive to relative phase and magnitude differences across harmonically relevant modes. It serves as the kernel for:

- Quantum Support Vector Machines (QSVM) for classification (e.g. crisis vs non-crisis).
- Quantum Kernel Regression (QKR) for regression targets (e.g. predicted volatility or spread changes).

### 5.5 Embedding climate–finance structure

The feature construction and mapping to qubits should respect the layer structure:

- Assign specific qubits or subspaces to different variable groups (drivers, climate, climate risk) or frequency bands (low-frequency, ENSO, annual/harmonic bands).
- Optionally introduce controlled-phase entangling gates between qubit groups corresponding to different layers or harmonic bands to encode cross-layer or cross-frequency couplings.

Formally, one can write
\[
U_{\mathrm{HPE}}(x) = U_{\mathrm{intra}}(x) \cdot U_{\mathrm{inter}}(x),
\]
where \(U_{\mathrm{intra}}\) is block-diagonal across layers/bands and \(U_{\mathrm{inter}}\) introduces structured cross-layer phase interactions.

### 5.6 Classical approximations and simulation

For implementation on classical hardware or noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices, one can either:

- Simulate the kernel exactly using the analytical overlap expression above.
- Approximate \(K_Q(x,x')\) with randomized Fourier features or other classical schemes for benchmarking, treating the quantum kernel as a structured, harmonically aware kernel family.

This separates the conceptual quantum design from its practical instantiation and allows direct comparison with classical spectral kernels.

## 6. Phase 7 – Predictive Modeling Blueprint

Phase 7 defines the predictive tasks, model classes, and evaluation strategy, with all elements expressed at a mathematical level so that coding can proceed in a straightforward manner.

### 6.1 Prediction targets and horizons

Let \(Y_t\) denote financial risk targets derived from \(R_t^{\mathrm{fin}}\). Typical choices include:

- One-step or multi-step ahead realized volatility of a risk index (e.g. VIX-like measures).
- Changes in credit spreads or CDS indices.
- Indicator variables for financial stress episodes (e.g. top quantile of volatility or spread changes).

Formally, define:

- Regression targets: \(y_t^{(\mathrm{reg})} = g(R_{t+H}^{\mathrm{fin}}, \dots, R_{t+H+H'}^{\mathrm{fin}})\), such as log-volatility or spread changes over horizon \(H\).
- Classification targets: \(y_t^{(\mathrm{cls})} \in \{0,1\}\) indicating whether a stress event occurs in a future window.

### 6.2 Feature–target pairs

For a given window center \(t_c\), construct:

- Spectral input feature: \(x(t_c)\) or \(\phi(t_c)\) from Phase 3.
- Quantum state: \(|\psi(x(t_c))\rangle\) from Phase 6.
- Target: \(y_{t_c}\) based on future \(R^{\mathrm{fin}}\) as above.

Collect these into training and test sets \(\{(x_i, y_i)\}\) indexed by windows.

### 6.3 Model classes

The framework supports three broad model classes:

1. **Classical spectral regression/classification**
   - Linear/logistic regression on \(x_i\).
   - Kernel regression/SVM with classical kernels (e.g. RBF, polynomial, spectral kernels built from \(\omega\)-space distances).
   - Neural networks with spectral inputs.

2. **Harmonic-structured spectral models**
   - Models where parameters are tied or regularized across harmonically related frequencies (e.g. shared weights for \(\omega\) and its multiples).
   - Multi-task formulations where prediction at different horizons or for different financial variables share harmonic representations.

3. **Quantum kernel models**
   - QSVM with kernel \(K_Q(x_i,x_j) = |\langle \psi(x_i) | \psi(x_j) \rangle|^2\).
   - QKR with the same kernel, minimizing squared error or other loss.

The project’s novelty lies in **comparing** these models within the same spectral–harmonic feature space, isolating the contribution of the quantum harmonic embedding.

### 6.4 Loss functions and training objectives

For regression, given predictions \(\hat y_i\) and targets \(y_i\), use:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{reg}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \hat y_i)^2 + \lambda \mathcal{R}(\theta),
\]
where \(\theta\) represents model parameters (e.g. support vectors, weights), \(\lambda\) is a regularization parameter, and \(\mathcal{R}\) a norm or harmonic-structure penalty.

For binary classification with QSVM or classical SVM, the hinge loss (or its regularized variant) applies:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cls}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \max\bigl\{0, 1 - y_i f(x_i)\bigr\} + \lambda \lVert f \rVert^2_{\mathcal{H}_K},
\]
where \(y_i \in \{-1,1\}\) and \(f\) lies in an RKHS associated with kernel \(K\) (classical or quantum).

### 6.5 Evaluation metrics

For regression tasks:

- Mean-squared error (MSE) and mean absolute error (MAE) on held-out windows.
- Out-of-sample \(R^2\) relative to naive benchmarks (e.g. random walk, historical mean).
- Frequency-conditional performance: MSE computed conditionally on periods dominated by particular harmonic modes (e.g. strong ENSO episodes).[11][1]

For classification tasks:

- Area under the ROC curve (AUC).
- Precision–recall metrics, especially for rare crisis events.
- Calibration plots for probabilistic outputs.

### 6.6 Model comparison dimensions

To assess the added value of harmonics and quantum embeddings, organize a systematic comparison along:

- **Representation axis:** time-domain vs global spectral vs localized harmonic spectral features.
- **Model axis:** linear vs nonlinear classical kernels vs quantum kernels.
- **Frequency axis:** performance conditional on specific harmonic bands (annual, ENSO, low-frequency, etc.).

This structure defines the experimental grid for Phase 8 (validation) and Phase 9 (interpretation) once coding and empirical work start.

## 7. Coding Readiness and Implementation Notes

With Phases 3–7 specified at the mathematical level, the transition to coding comprises the following concrete steps (not executed here, but implied by the design):

1. Implement the Phase 2 preprocessing operators \(\mathcal{A}_j, \mathcal{I}_j, \mathcal{S}_j, \mathcal{N}_j\) to obtain \(\hat Z_t\).
2. Compute windowed DFTs, spectral density estimates, and cross-spectral quantities to build feature vectors \(\phi(t_c)\) and frequency-domain objects \(\hat S_Z(\omega)\), \(\hat H(\omega)\).
3. Estimate the Harmonic Spectral Transmission Operator \(\mathcal{T}_H(\omega)\) and harmonic network \(W(\omega)\).
4. Implement the harmonic quantum embedding map \(x \mapsto |\psi(x)\rangle\) and the associated kernel \(K_Q\), either via quantum simulation or analytic formulas.
5. Train and compare classical and quantum models on the defined prediction tasks using the specified loss functions and evaluation metrics.

No coding details are included here; instead, the design aims to be sufficiently explicit that implementation in Python (NumPy/SciPy for spectral analysis, scikit-learn and Qiskit/PennyLane for classical and quantum models) can follow directly.
